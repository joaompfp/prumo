# Historical Energy Prices — Research Summary

> Mário J. S. Brito | 2022-10-18
> Source PDF: HistoricalEnergyPricesfortheEuropeanUnionandUnitedStatesofAmerica.pdf

## 1. What the paper is

This is a chart-heavy comparative study of historical energy prices in the European Union and the United States, with a deeper focus on Portugal as a case study.

The author standardizes prices into comparable units, updates monetary values to constant prices (mostly €2021), and compares consumer-level prices across residential, commercial, and industrial segments for:

- Electricity
- Natural gas
- Road fuels
- Related context via GDP per capita and inflation

The paper is mostly descriptive. It explicitly says it does not aim to provide a full causal discussion or a formal conclusion.

## 2. Scope and methodology

- Main time window: roughly 1985–2021
- Some series extend back to 1900–1920
- Prices are converted to a common currency and adjusted for inflation
- EU figures use representative statistical conventions that may differ from US series conventions
- Portugal is used as a detailed case study because it is a smaller Western European economy with a long energy-transition history

The author repeatedly warns that the data has uncertainty and should be read cautiously.

## 3. Main takeaways

### Electricity

- Residential electricity prices in the EU and US are relatively close until the early 2000s.
- After about 2003, EU prices rise while US prices flatten.
- The paper links the US pattern to shale gas expansion and fuel switching in power generation.
- EU countries show large internal variation, with some major economies following smoother trajectories than others.

### Natural gas

- Industrial natural gas prices in the US are consistently lower than in Europe.
- The gap widens after 2007.
- The US pattern is again associated with shale gas and lower domestic gas costs.

### Road fuels

- EU and US road-fuel prices move in broadly parallel fashion.
- Diesel is generally cheaper than gasoline in the comparative figures.
- Taxes, regulation, and energy policy appear to be major drivers of differences.

### GDP per capita context

- The paper adds GDP per capita figures in comparable terms to help interpret affordability and relative burden.
- The idea is not just to compare absolute prices, but price relative to wealth.

## 4. US state comparison

The US section compares representative states such as Alaska, California, Hawaii, Texas, Washington, and others.

Observed pattern:

- Most states cluster around the national curve
- Hawaii is a persistent high-price outlier, especially for natural gas
- The post-2007 drop in gas prices is associated with shale gas and regional market differences

## 5. Portugal case study

Portugal gets the deepest treatment.

The paper frames Portugal as a useful case for understanding energy scarcity, industrialization, and transition under pressure.

Key historical themes:

- Wartime shortages and firewood dependence during World War II
- Post-war electrification and hydroelectric expansion
- Oil shocks of the 1970s and early 1980s as major stress events
- Economic and political instability in the aftermath of the 1970s crisis period
- Strong growth in transport demand and motorization after 1960
- Wind and solar expansion from the 2000s onward

The Portuguese section is meant to show how prices and energy policy interact over decades in a smaller EU economy exposed to external energy shocks.

## 6. How useful this is for Prumo

This paper is useful as background reference when Prumo needs:

- Historical price context for Portugal vs EU vs US
- Long-run framing for electricity, gas, and fuel affordability
- Supporting narrative for energy transition charts or commentary
- A source that justifies using Portugal as a case study in broader European comparisons

It is less useful as a causal or policy-evaluation paper, because the author keeps the treatment mostly descriptive.

## 7. Caveat

Do not treat this as a tight econometric argument. It is a comparative historical atlas of prices, with explanatory notes, not a formal hypothesis-testing paper.
